Business Transformation
Using technology to break down technological barriers

BI is evolving more quickly than ever before. Cloud, mobility, big data, the Internet of Things, artificial intelligence, and analytics are some of the technological innovations propelling this shift. Concurrently, as competition rises and regulatory constraints tighten (particularly in sectors like BFSI, Retail, and HLS), there is an increasing need for user data and insights. Legacy technology is the main obstacle to keeping up with this pace of change.

The demand is too great for platforms and systems implemented six or seven years ago. These systems lack scalability and flexibility, are difficult to integrate with other novel applications and third-party systems, are expensive to maintain, and many lack end-of-life support. These indicate that the time is right for your BI platform.

The earlier, the better.

Organizations are searching for structured methods for streamlining the management of their BI platforms to overcome the aforementioned challenges. These methods involve unifying the BI tools across the enterprise, rationalizing the current report inventory, migrating to a newer BI platform, and then establishing BI governance. Delaying the shift comes at a high cost. According to one study, employees in companies with poor BI tool consolidation use BI tools at a lower rate (~12%) than employees in companies with strong BI standardization and consolidation (~25%). Because of this, BI standardization is much more expensive per employee. Additionally, the survey discovered that companies with standardized BI tools spend 36% less on BI as a share of revenue on average.

These findings have far-reaching consequences, indicating that there is a hidden cost to postponing BI transformation that many firms are unaware of: fewer people use BI in the absence of simplification and consolidation, which results in missed opportunities and operational inefficiencies.

Framework of BI Transformation

The secret to a successful BI transformation is to take into account BI consolidation and use strong migration frameworks, tools, and accelerators to address simplification, standardization, and governance procedures holistically. A flexible, extendable, and configurable BI platform should be the ultimate product. There is no denying the benefits of uniformity and consolidation. To accomplish this, businesses need to take four easy steps:

Selecting and standardizing tools

Collaborate with your business and IT heads to comprehend current and future business intelligence requirements. This facilitates the selection of appropriate technology stacks and unifies BI into a single, top-tier BI platform. Due to reduced support, maintenance, and governance of fewer BI products, this immediately improves the reliability of data by 20 to 25% of enterprise semantic models and lowers TCO by 30%. Following their selection, the instruments aid in defining the governance that surrounds them.

Rationalization of Reports

Because of the various BI platforms, enterprises have a large number of reports without any way to monitor their effectiveness. In order to identify comparable or duplicate reports, as well as "Unused/Unuseful" reports, and to rationalize them, organizations must record their inventory of reports using a systematic and automated approach. Generally, 40â€“50% of reports are identified as potential candidates for rationalization, and removing them can result in a 25% reduction in generation and maintenance costs.

Platform Transformation

Dashboards, procedures, and patterns are familiar to users. How are they going to react to change? Platform transformation has two components: user-centered change management and technology migration. To ensure low mistake rates and short time-to-market, it is best to develop a migration framework that incorporates automation. It is essential to extract metadata from current systems and map it to the intended platform. Data transformation from your system to any targeted system should be possible. It is also advised that change management procedures be implemented early on by educating users about the technical advantages, raising their awareness of the simplification, and minimizing their discomfort.

Definition of Governance and Roadmap

A good BI transformation plan consists of both a roadmap and governance (policies and procedures). These must be in line with technology adoption, organizational process maturity, and users. For instance, based on technological settings and industry/regulatory constraints, on-premise, cloud, and hybrid models will require distinct governance procedures. It is impossible to undervalue the importance of sound government. When they have effective governance in place, many firms see a 20% increase in benefit realization.
